Your Questions, Answered
Should I use the FTA waiver to get rid of the $600 penalty my CPA caused?
Short Answer: Yes — 100%

The First-Time Abatement waiver exists precisely for this situation: a taxpayer with a clean history who got hit with a penalty that wasn't really their fault. Using it is not a gray area — it's a legitimate, IRS-sanctioned process that the IRS itself will walk you through on the phone.

Here's why you should absolutely use it:

  • The penalty was caused by your CPA's late filing, not by you intentionally dodging taxes
  • The FTA is a one-time use — this is exactly the moment it's designed for
  • There is no negative consequence for requesting it. The worst they can say is no
  • $600 is real money. Getting it back takes a single phone call
  • Using the FTA does not "use up" any future rights — it resets after 3 clean filing years

One important note: ideally your CPA should be making this call on your behalf since the late filing was their error. You can ask them directly: "Can you call the IRS and request an FTA for the late filing penalty?" If they refuse or stall, make the call yourself — it's straightforward.

Call 1-800-829-1040 and say: "I'd like to request a First-Time Penalty Abatement for tax year [year]. I have a clean penalty history for the prior three years."

How do I approach my CPA so they take responsibility and reduce my workload?
CPA Accountability

The key is to be direct, factual, and unapologetic — you're not asking a favor, you're asking them to do their job. A good CPA will respond well to this. A bad one will reveal themselves quickly.

Use this script:

"Hey [name], I've been reviewing my tax situation and I noticed the late filing generated a $600 IRS penalty. Since the delay was on the filing side, I think it's fair for you to call the IRS and request the First-Time Penalty Abatement on my behalf — that's a standard call any CPA can make. Can you handle that this week?"

How to reduce your workload going forward:

  • Ask them to send you a checklist in January of exactly what documents you need to gather — no back and forth
  • Ask them to reach out to you proactively before deadlines, not the other way around
  • Ask them to explain every line of your return in plain language, not tax jargon
  • Ask: "Are there any credits or deductions I might be missing?" — they should already be doing this without being asked

If they push back on the FTA call:

  • Remind them it's a 10-minute IRS phone call, not a complex task
  • If they still won't do it — that's a red flag and a signal to find someone new
  • A CPA who won't fix their own mistake isn't worth keeping
What are the signs of a good CPA, and how do I find one?
Finding a Great CPA

At your income level ($30K, single, California) you don't need the most expensive CPA — you need a proactive, communicative one who treats your situation seriously. Here's exactly what to look for.

Green flags — signs of a great CPA:

  • Reaches out to you before deadlines, not after
  • Explains every decision in plain English without being asked
  • Proactively mentions credits and deductions (EITC, IRA, Saver's Credit) without you having to ask
  • Asks about life changes: new job, side income, moving, student loans
  • Files on time, every time — or contacts you early if they need more info
  • Takes ownership when something goes wrong and fixes it without argument
  • Available for quick questions between tax seasons

Red flags — signs to walk away:

  • Only contacts you once a year at tax time
  • Files late and doesn't proactively fix the resulting penalty
  • Rushes through your return without explaining anything
  • Has never mentioned EITC, FTA, or retirement contributions to you
  • Charges extra for every small question or follow-up
  • Can't clearly explain why a number looks the way it does

How to verify and find one:

  • CPAverify.org — verify any CPA's license status before hiring
  • NATP.org — National Association of Tax Professionals directory
  • Ask friends in a similar financial situation (self-employed, similar income) for referrals
  • Interview 2–3 CPAs before choosing — a good one will offer a free 15-min consult
  • For your income level, expect to pay $200–$400 for a federal + state return. If someone charges significantly more without explanation, ask why

Bottom line: The best CPA for you is someone who makes tax season feel easy, not like homework. If you leave every interaction more confused than before, that's the wrong person.
